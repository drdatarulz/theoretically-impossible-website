The Network You Don't Know You Have

March 2026 · Kevin Phifer

I work from home. I've worked from home for fifteen, maybe twenty years. My daily world is an office in my house. Some days I don't leave the house at all. Other days I realize I haven't left in two days and force myself to go somewhere — anywhere — just to remember what outside looks like.

I take meetings on Teams and WebEx. I was doing this before COVID made it mainstream. My teams have always been distributed — mostly across the United States, sometimes across the world — but my physical world is small. It's this room, this desk, this screen.

So I've always assumed my professional network was small too.

The Moment It Hit Me

When I left my last organization to start Theoretically Impossible Solutions, one of my colleagues — a guy named Bob who'd been on my team for a couple of years — said something that stopped me cold.

"What am I going to do?"

I laughed and told him what I always tell people: improvise, adapt, and overcome. But he wasn't joking. He said, "You always have these people you call on. We run into trouble, and you just — you know a guy."

And I realized he was right. Of everyone I'd worked with at that company, Bob had probably benefited the most from my network. But until he said it out loud, I'd never really thought of it as a network at all.

The People You Accumulate

When I started counting, it surprised me.

Bob needed a senior developer. I knew Nahush — we'd worked together years earlier, and I had a sense he was ready for something new. I made the introduction, Bob hired him, and it was a great fit. That's one.

Then there was the vendor situation. A software vendor we relied on decided they were done supporting their product. They'd originally built it thinking they could sell it to the masses, but we turned out to be their only customer. They wanted to dump the code in our lap and walk away. We needed to build a team to take it over, and it was written in Spring Boot — a tech stack none of us had deep experience with, since we were largely a .NET shop.

So I called Skyler. Smartest guy I know — don't tell him I said that. He consulted with us on the architecture. That's two.

We still needed someone to actually develop on it. I remembered Volker — a guy I'd worked with years before. We'd never been on the same team exactly. The way I'd describe it is my team would crash his systems. We'd overload something, he'd call and say "can you guys throttle that down?" But I knew he was good, and I had a sense he might be between jobs. I pinged him on LinkedIn, asked if he was looking, and connected him with Bob. He became our first Spring Boot developer. That's three.

Then we hit a scaling problem. The whole system ran on Docker containers in AWS, but we were an Azure shop, and the infrastructure team didn't have the experience to diagnose why containers were scaling wrong — doubling load on certain units instead of distributing it properly. Nobody on the team knew where to even start.

But I remembered my buddy BJ. Last I'd heard, he'd gone to work for AWS. BJ and I have one of those friendships where we'll have coffee every week for months, sharing war stories and ideas, and then life happens and we don't talk for years. No hard feelings — it just falls off the radar. Then something comes up and we pick right back up like no time has passed.

So I'm sitting on a Teams call with the team, staring at this scaling problem, and I just text BJ: "Hey, you still at AWS?" Turns out he'd moved on, but he obviously had the expertise. I asked if he could jump on the call, he did, and he helped us get to the root of it. We ended up contracting with him to solve the whole thing. That's four.

Nahush. Skyler. Volker. BJ. All from one project, all from one colleague's experience of working with me. And that was just Bob's world.

It's Not Who You Know

You hear it all the time: "It's not what you know, it's who you know." I've always found that a little too simple. After thirty years in this industry, I'd put a different spin on it.

It's not who you know. It's who knows you.

The job I just left? I didn't go looking for it. Someone reached out to a person in my network and asked if she could take on a project. She said no — but she said, "You should call Kevin." That's how it started. Not because I was networking. Not because I was on LinkedIn optimizing my profile. Because someone who knew my work thought of me when the right opportunity came up.

That's a fundamentally different thing than "networking." Networking is deliberate. It's going to events, collecting business cards, sending follow-up emails. I'm not knocking it — it works for some people. But what I'm describing is something that builds itself over the course of a career, almost without you noticing.

Every time you solve a hard problem with someone, you're building it. Every time you're the person who says "I know a guy" and that guy delivers, you're building it. Every time you're good to work with — not just competent, but reliable, honest, the kind of person people want to call when things go sideways — you're building it.

You just don't see it, because your world feels like one room in one house.

Why This Matters Now

I'm telling this story because starting Theoretically Impossible Solutions forced me to confront something I'd been blind to. When you work for someone else, your network operates in the background. You call on people when you need them, and you don't think about it structurally. It's just how you get things done.

But when you start your own company, that network becomes something else entirely. It becomes your bench. Your referral pipeline. Your consulting roster. The people who will vouch for you when a potential client asks, "Has this guy actually done this before?"

I spent decades accumulating these relationships without ever thinking of them as an asset. I'd tell you I had one friend, maybe two. Meanwhile, I could pick up the phone and solve problems across four different technology stacks, three cloud platforms, and a dozen specialized domains — all because of people who knew me, trusted me, and picked up when I called.

If you're an engineer who's been doing this for a while, you probably have this too. You just haven't counted. The people you've worked with, the ones you've helped, the ones who've helped you, the ones you'd call at a moment's notice if something was on fire — that's your network. It's bigger than you think. And if you ever decide to go out on your own, it's one of the most valuable things you'll bring with you.

Bob knew it before I did. Sometimes it takes someone else pointing it out.
